Mataian Barrier Lake — Hualien (2025)

Overview Facts Climate Link Timeline Sources

When a mountain dammed a river

A massive landslide created a barrier lake upstream of Mataian Creek in Hualien County. When it failed during extreme rainfall, it caused devastating flooding downstream.

Overview

This page summarizes the Mataian Creek barrier-lake event in Hualien (2025), explains why barrier lakes form, and links the disaster to increasing extreme rainfall under climate change.

6 Key Facts

  1. Barrier lake formation: The lake was formed by a landslide that blocked the river upstream of Guangfu, Hualien.
  2. Huge volume: At its peak the barrier lake held an estimated tens of millions of cubic meters of water (on the order of ~90 million m³).
  3. Failure during typhoon rain: Heavy rains associated with Typhoon Ragasa (Sept 2025) caused the lake to overflow and partially fail, releasing a massive wave of water, mud and debris downstream.
  4. Losses: The flood caused many casualties and widespread property damage in Guangfu and nearby towns; rescue and cleanup involved thousands of volunteers.
  5. Why it is dangerous: Barrier-lake failures can produce sudden, high-energy flows that carry boulders and sediment — far more destructive than ordinary river floods.
  6. Climate link: Extreme precipitation events have become more frequent/intense in the region, increasing the risk that heavy rains will trigger landslides and dam formation.

Climate Change Connections

  • Warmer atmosphere → more moisture → stronger short-duration rainfall bursts that trigger landslides.
  • Repeated extreme storms (multiple typhoons in a season) can create landslide-prone conditions and fill newly formed barrier lakes quickly.
  • Mountainous Taiwan is especially vulnerable: steep slopes + concentrated rainfall = high landslide and barrier-lake risk.
